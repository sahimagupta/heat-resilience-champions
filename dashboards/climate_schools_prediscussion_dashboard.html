📊 What the OECD data shows — direct impact on children's learning
📉
USA: Each 1°F (0.55°C) hotter school year without air conditioning reduces PSAT scores by ~1%. Compounding year on year.
📝
Japan: An additional school day above 34°C without air conditioning lowers test scores by 0.56 standard deviations.
🏥
London (UK): Hospital admissions increased 0.24% among children under 5 on high-heat days.
🤒
England: 2.5% increase in the proportion of fever calls on high-temperature days.
🏫
France 2025: Nearly 1,900 schools closed. Southern Brazil suspended return to classes. 18 Mexican states restricted outdoor activities (2023).

By 2050 — what children in primary school will face
Under a middle-of-the-road scenario, students across Bulgaria, Hungary, Colombia, Costa Rica and the USA face more than 30–60 extra days per year above 30°C at school by 2050. Under high emissions, this reaches 60–365 days across most OECD nations.
CHC-CMIP6 · 13 countries · OECD ad hoc survey
OECD INSIGHT
The adaptation gap — what OECD governments are doing
Japan: universalised AC in all public schools. Australia: AUD $477M invested to equip 649 state schools. England: mapped overheating risks in schools. Paris: assessed 670 school grounds via geolocation. Rhode Island: built 4 contingency heat days into the calendar. Spain: shifted classes to early morning during heatwaves.

Nearly invisible in global curricula
The compounding gap: Less climate content appears in social science than in science syllabi. Less appears in Grade 3 than in Grade 6 or 9. The youngest — and most formative — learners get the least. And zero climate finance has been directed to education to fix it. (UNESCO GEM Report 2024)
