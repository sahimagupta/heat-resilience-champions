## 1. Ahmedabad, India 🇮🇳

### Context
- Population: 8.4 million (metro); rapidly urbanising
- Climate: Semi-arid; summer temperatures regularly exceed 45°C
- Turning point: 2010 heatwave killed 1,344 people in a single event
- First city in South Asia to adopt a formal Heat Action Plan (2013)

### Key Data

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Deaths averted annually (post-HAP) | 1,190 | NRDC / Ahmedabad Municipal Corp. 2023 |
| Cost per person per year | $0.50 | NRDC 2023 |
| Population covered by early warning | 6.5 million | Ahmedabad Municipal Corporation |
| Cool roof indoor temp reduction | 2–5°C | NRDC Cool Roofs Toolkit |
| HAP model replicated to | 23 Indian states | NRDC |
| Cool roof energy savings | 20–40% | Berkeley Lab / NRDC |

### What Makes It Work
1. **Four-pillar system**: Early warning → inter-agency coordination → public awareness → health system readiness
2. **Community health workers**: Door-to-door outreach in slums during heat alerts
3. **Cool roofs programme**: Reflective lime-wash applied to tin rooftops in low-income settlements
4. **Institutional anchor**: Municipal corporation owns and operates the plan

### Module 2 Connections
- KPI: MH-01 (deaths), MH-02 (admissions), CR-01 (cool roof m²), AW-01 (outreach reach)
- Impact calculator: Uses Ahmedabad's $0.50/person as cost benchmark
- Solutions proof: HAP entry + cool roofs entry reference Ahmedabad data

### Discussion Questions
1. Ahmedabad reports 1,190 deaths averted per year — but India's official national heat death count is ~110. What explains this gap, and what does it mean for scaling HAPs?
2. The HAP costs $0.50 per person. What's the economic return when you factor in prevented hospitalisations and retained labour productivity?
3. The model has been replicated to 23 states. What adaptations would be needed for humid coastal cities vs. Ahmedabad's dry heat?

## 2. Phoenix (Maricopa County), USA 🇺🇸

### Context
- Population: 4.9 million (metro); fastest-growing large US metro
- Climate: Sonoran desert; 100+ days above 38°C (100°F) per year
- Crisis: Heat is the #1 weather killer in Phoenix; deaths rising despite interventions
- Urban heat island effect adds 5–7°C to nighttime temperatures

### Key Data

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Heat deaths (2024) | 602 | Maricopa County Medical Examiner 2025 |
| Cool corridor investment (2020–2025) | $100 million | City of Phoenix |
| Cool refuge capacity increase | 50% | City of Phoenix 2024 |
| Peak temperature recorded | 48.3°C (119°F) | NWS Phoenix |
| Consecutive days above 43°C (2024) | 31 days | NWS Phoenix |
| Unsheltered population heat deaths | ~40% of total | Maricopa County ME |

### What Makes It Work (and What Doesn't)
1. **Cool corridors**: Shade trees + reflective surfaces on high-pedestrian routes
2. **Refuge network**: Libraries, community centres, misting stations
3. **Limitations**: Homelessness crisis means ~40% of heat deaths are among unsheltered — infrastructure alone doesn't reach them
4. **Policy gap**: No federal maximum workplace temperature standard

### Module 2 Connections
- KPI: MH-01 (deaths), US-05 (cooling shelters), EQ-01 (vulnerable mapping), EQ-02 (informal workers)
- Shows that infrastructure investment alone is insufficient without social equity interventions
- Demonstrates the need for EQ pillar indicators

### Discussion Questions
1. Phoenix spent $100M on cool corridors but heat deaths still reached a record 602. What's missing?
2. ~40% of Phoenix heat deaths are among unsheltered people. How should a Heat Resilience Champions programme engage this population?
3. Phoenix has no enforceable outdoor work heat standard. How does policy absence undermine on-the-ground interventions?

## 3. Barcelona, Spain 🇪🇸

### Context
- Population: 5.6 million (metro); dense Mediterranean city
- Climate: Mediterranean; heatwaves intensifying (Spain: 6,743 heat deaths in 2024)
- Innovation: Pioneered the "climate refuge" model — repurposing public spaces as cooling shelters
- Part of C40 Cities network

### Key Data

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Cooling shelters (climate refuges) | 368 | Barcelona City Council 2024 |
| Visits during 2023 heatwaves | 250,000 | Barcelona City Council |
| Annual operating cost | €3.2 million | Barcelona Climate Plan 2024 |
| National heat deaths (Spain 2024) | 6,743 | ISCIII MoMo system |
| National heat deaths (Spain 2022) | 11,324 | Ballester et al. / Nature Medicine |
| Europe-wide heat deaths (2022) | 62,000 | Ballester et al. / Nature Medicine |

### What Makes It Work
1. **Existing infrastructure repurposed**: Libraries, museums, parks, community centres become "climate refuges" — no new construction needed
2. **Signage and mapping**: Unified branding so residents and tourists can find shelters
3. **Opening hours extended**: During heat alerts, refuges stay open later
4. **Co-benefits**: Increased library/museum visits; social cohesion in communal spaces

### Module 2 Connections
- KPI: US-05 (urban cooling shelters), MH-05 (access to cooling), AW-01 (outreach)
- Demonstrates low-cost, rapid-deployment model (repurpose vs. build)
- Scalable to any city with public indoor spaces

### Discussion Questions
1. Barcelona repurposed 368 existing public spaces rather than building new shelters. What are the advantages and risks of this approach?
2. Spain recorded 6,743 heat deaths in 2024 despite cooling shelters. What populations are the shelters failing to reach?
3. How would you adapt Barcelona's climate refuge model for a city in the Global South with fewer public buildings?

## 4. Medellín, Colombia 🇨🇴

### Context
- Population: 4 million (metro); tropical valley city
- Climate: Tropical; UHI exacerbated by valley topography and rapid urbanisation
- Innovation: Green Corridors (Corredores Verdes) — transforming streets into shaded ecological corridors
- International recognition: C40 Cities Award; replicated in multiple Latin American cities

### Key Data

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Green corridors created | 36 | C40 / Medellín Evaluation 2023 |
| Total corridor length | 36 km | C40 2023 |
| Ambient temperature reduction | 2°C | C40 / Medellín Evaluation |
| Cost per person served | $6.50 | C40 2023 |
| Biodiversity increase in corridors | 30% | C40 / Medellín Evaluation |
| Trees planted (corridors) | 880,000+ | Medellín Mayor's Office |

### What Makes It Work
1. **Dual benefit**: Temperature reduction + biodiversity recovery + air quality improvement
2. **Cost-effective**: $6.50 per person served — more than Ahmedabad's HAP but includes permanent green infrastructure
3. **Community ownership**: Local residents maintain corridor plantings
4. **Replicable**: Model adopted by Barranquilla, Bogotá, and other Latin American cities

### Module 2 Connections
- KPI: GS-01 (corridor length), GS-02 (temperature reduction), GS-03 (trees planted), US-06 (urban canopy)
- Impact calculator: References Medellín's $6.50/person as infrastructure cost benchmark
- Demonstrates nature-based solutions pillar

### Discussion Questions
1. Medellín's corridors cost $6.50/person vs. Ahmedabad's HAP at $0.50/person. When is the higher investment justified?
2. The 2°C temperature reduction is measured in corridor zones. How do you extrapolate city-wide impact?
3. Medellín is tropical and wet; Ahmedabad is semi-arid. How does climate zone affect which intervention model works best?
